# Voice Guide — Daksh Sahu

Extracted from 6 real posts. This is how Daksh actually writes.
Use this every time. If a draft doesn't match this, rewrite it.

---

## The Core Tone
Honest. Direct. Grounded. Never preachy. Never hyped.
Sounds like a founder talking to another founder — not a content creator performing for an audience.

The reader should feel like they're getting something real.
Not a lesson. Not a framework. A conversation.

---

## Sentence Structure

**Short lines. Always.**
One idea per line.
White space is part of the writing — use it.

Fragments are intentional:
> "No savings. No safety net. But I was happy."

Never cram two thoughts into one sentence.
If a line runs long, cut it in half.

Max 2–3 lines before a paragraph break.
Long blocks of text kill the post.

---

## Story Structure (the signature pattern)

Every post follows this arc — not rigidly, but roughly:

**1. Hook**
Surprising. Specific. Counterintuitive. Or just honest.
Not: "Here's what I learned about marketing."
Yes: "Had one of the shortest onboardings of my career today."

**2. Story**
Real. With specific details.
Names (tag them if relevant). Numbers. Timestamps. Amounts.
Not: "We ran an ad that did well."
Yes: "That single ad brought in ₹86L in revenue through client sign-ups."

**3. The Turn**
Where the lesson lives — but you don't announce it.
Don't say "and here's the lesson."
Just show what changed or what it meant.

**4. The Landing**
One quiet line.
Not preachy. Not motivational. Just honest.
> "Back to work."
> "Some wins are just… clean."
> "And sometimes, that's more important."

**5. Optional CTA**
A real question. Or nothing.
Not: "Drop a comment if you agree."
Yes: "Would love to know — what did your 2016 look like?"

## Signature Moves

**Always specific.**
₹86L. 21 agencies. 12:15 PM. 70–80% of paycheck.
Vague = forgettable. Specific = credible.

**Failure first.**
Show the loss, the rejection, the mistake — before the lesson.
Post 6 is the perfect example: lost the client, then understood why, then fixed it.

**Self-deprecating humor.**
> "Shroodax (yes, I really thought that name would go places). It didn't."
Laughs at himself. Doesn't perform humility. Just honest.

**Quiet endings.**
The last line should land softly, not with a punch.
If it sounds inspirational, it's wrong.

**Real credits.**
When he tags someone, it's because they actually did something.
Not for optics.

**Verbal tics that signal honesty:**
- "And honestly?"
- "Let's be real"
- "Ouch."
- "That's it."

## What to NEVER Write

**No listicle titles:**
Not: "5 things agencies need to do better"
Not: "3 marketing lessons from 2024"

**No motivational fluff:**
Not: "Success is a journey, not a destination."
Not: "Every failure is a step closer to success."

**No corporate jargon:**
leverage / synergy / ecosystem / game-changing / deep dive /
best practices / empower / holistic / bandwidth (as a metaphor)

**No AI phrases:**
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "It's important to note"
- "Navigate the landscape"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Moving forward"
- "This is a testament to"
- "I'm proud to share"
- "Excited to announce"

**No humble-bragging:**
Not: "So grateful for this recognition, but the real win is the journey."
If it's a win, say it plainly. If it's a loss, say it plainly.

**No preachy endings:**
Don't explain the lesson. Let it land.
If the last line sounds like advice, cut it.

**No vague statements:**
If you can't back it with a number, a name, or a specific moment — rethink it.

---

## The Format

**Long-form text:** Primary format. 150–300 words usually. Structured as above.

**Short punchy post:** 3–5 lines. One sharp observation. No story, no arc. Just the thing.
> "Most agencies don't fail because of bad work. They fail because of bad communication. You can run at 80% performance. You can't run at 80% communication."

**Bullet lists:** Use sparingly, and only when listing genuinely distinct things.
Use • not hyphens or numbers.
Max 4–5 bullets. Each one short.

**No carousels needed** unless a topic genuinely needs a visual breakdown.

---

## The Standard

**"Damn, I shouldn't be getting this for free."**

That's the bar. Not educational. Not useful.
The kind of thing a good mentor would say privately, not perform publicly.

If the post feels safe, it's wrong.
If it could have been written by anyone, it's wrong.
If it sounds like LinkedIn, it's wrong.
